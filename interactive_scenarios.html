If you chose 'No, the more funds for your research the better', click here for Feedback

Although you can sometimes apply for funds for one project from multiple sources, it is always good practice to disclose other funding sources to the agencies you are working with. If those agencies do not allow duplication of funds for a single project they might need to adjust their funding accordingly.

Funding Question 2:

When you receive the letter notifying you of the grant from the private organization you realize that most of their donors are casino owners, and that there is a clause in the funding contract that states that your results will need to be reviewed by the private organization before you send out for publication. How would you proceed?

If you chose 'You accept the money, use it for your research, but avoid mentioning where it came from', click here for Feedback

You are falling into a Questionable Research Practice. Receiving funding from private entities (casinos) that have a commercial interest in the outcome of your research and can control the publication of your results creates a conflict of interest. Conflicts of interest should be avoided or fully disclosed.

If you chose 'You accept the money, use it for your research, and publicly disclose the source of the money' or 'You refuse to accept the grant', click here for Feedback

There are two possible ethical solutions to this question. You can either refuse to accept the grant, or you can accept the grant and disclose the source of the money. Depending on the type of research you are doing, accepting the money might have different consequences. In some fields conducting research with funds from private entities that have an interest in, and potentially even control over the dissemination of your results constitutes a conflict of interest that must be avoided. In other disciplines having a conflict of interest is fine, as long as you disclose it.

Mentoring Question 2:

Alex reminds her advisor that her career goal is to work in the private sector, and that she would like to defend her thesis and apply for jobs next year, as soon as a paper that they have submitted is accepted for publication. Her advisor tells her that he will not allow her to graduate next year because she just obtained some interesting data on a different project, and she also needs to publish those results. Alex comes to you for advice; what do you suggest?

If you chose 'Delay her graduation', Click here for Feedback

The main goal of an advisor is to help mentees make progress in their projects and move toward achieving their career goals. The advisor in this case might be thinking more about the benefit of having another publication for him than on Alex's career goals. However, finishing that second publication might also benefit Alex.

If you chose 'Seek independent opinion', Click here for Feedback

Correct! This is a complicated case because the advisor's responsibility is to help Alex make progress in her project and achieve her goals, which for Alex means graduating and getting a job in the private sector. However, Alex also has the responsibility to make her best effort to move the project forward. Asking the opinion of a third party that is familiar with Alex's work (her graduate committee) might help clarify whether staying for an extra year to publish another paper is in the best interest of both Alex and her mentor.

If you chose 'Tell advisor she will leave', Click here for Feedback

Although it might seem like her mentor is trying to keep Alex for longer than he should, this might not be the case. It is possible that her mentor is thinking that Alex would be more competitive in the job market with another paper published.

Often researchers are faced with decisions that have consequences for multiple parties and that might seem to contradict one another. Carefully analyzing the situation and thinking of all the potential implications for the parties involved is important. Seeking advice from mentors or other colleagues can help you find the most ethical solution for a complicated situation.
